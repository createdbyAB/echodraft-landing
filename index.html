01What this is

A patient editor who carries a notebook.

Most writing tools hand you a blank page and a blinking cursor — the exact moment the thought goes quiet.

EchoDraft starts the other way around. You talk: on a walk, in the car, in the ten minutes before a meeting. It listens until you're empty, then asks one or two quiet questions — never a cheerleader, never two questions at once. When you stop, it reads back a draft worth keeping.

Not a transcript. Not bullet points. A blog post, a thread, a script — shaped, paragraphed, in a voice that still sounds like yours. The half-thought you had on the walk arrives at your desk already written down.

02How it works

Three steps, and none of them is staring.

Step one

Tap and talk.

Open the app to a microphone, not a cursor. Say the half-thought out loud — the one you'd lose by the time you sat down.

Step two

Answer one or two questions.

When you pause, a quiet reader nudges — "linger there, what's the part you're walking around?" You answer; it keeps listening.

Step three

Read it back.

Your rambling returns shaped and paragraphed — yours to edit, keep, or send. The draft you'd have written if the words had waited for the desk.

03Install on Android

Sideloading is unusual. This isn't.

EchoDraft isn't on the Play Store yet — during the beta you install it directly. Android will warn you, because that's its job. Here's why this one is safe to wave through.

Signed, reproducible build. Every release is signed with the same key and published openly — you can verify the fingerprint matches.
Hosted on GitHub Releases. Not a file locker, not a mirror. The APK comes straight from our public repository with a checksum.
Audio leaves only to draft. Recordings are processed to make your draft, then handled by your settings. Nothing is sold, ever.

04This is a beta

Early, on purpose. Honest about it.

EchoDraft is in private beta. Most of it works the way it should; some of it will surprise us both. You're here early because you talk things out before you can write them down — and because you'll tell us when something feels off.

Drafts can be rough. The reader occasionally asks the wrong question. If a recording is ever lost, that's the bug we most want to hear about.
